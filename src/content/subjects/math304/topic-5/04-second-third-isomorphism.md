# Second and Third Isomorphism Theorems

## Introduction

While the First Isomorphism Theorem is the most fundamental, the Second and Third Isomorphism Theorems provide additional powerful tools for understanding the structure of quotient groups. These theorems, along with the Correspondence Theorem, complete our understanding of how subgroups and quotients interact.

## Second Isomorphism Theorem (Diamond Isomorphism)

**Second Isomorphism Theorem**: Let $H$ be a subgroup of $G$ and let $N$ be a normal subgroup of $G$. Then:

1. $HN = \{hn : h \in H, n \in N\}$ is a subgroup of $G$
2. $H \cap N$ is a normal subgroup of $H$
3. $N$ is a normal subgroup of $HN$
4. $$H/(H \cap N) \cong HN/N$$

**Name**: Called the "Diamond" or "Parallelogram" theorem because of the diagram:

```
        HN
       /  \
      H    N
       \  /
       H∩N
        |
        {e}
```

### Proof

**Part 1**: Show $HN \leq G$.

We use the subgroup test for products of subgroups. First, note that:
$$HN \cdot HN = \{h_1 n_1 h_2 n_2 : h_i \in H, n_i \in N\}$$

Since $N \triangleleft G$, we have $n_1 h_2 = h_2 n'$ for some $n' \in N$ (by normality). Thus:
$$h_1 n_1 h_2 n_2 = h_1 h_2 n' n_2 \in HN$$

Also, $(hn)^{-1} = n^{-1}h^{-1} = h^{-1}(hn^{-1}h^{-1}) \in HN$ (again using normality).

Therefore $HN \leq G$. ✓

**Part 2**: Show $H \cap N \triangleleft H$.

Since $N \triangleleft G$, for any $h \in H$ and $x \in H \cap N$:
$$hxh^{-1} \in N \text{ (by normality of } N \text{)}$$
$$hxh^{-1} \in H \text{ (since } h, x \in H \text{)}$$

Therefore $hxh^{-1} \in H \cap N$, proving $H \cap N \triangleleft H$. ✓

**Part 3**: $N \triangleleft HN$ follows since $N \triangleleft G$ and $HN \subseteq G$. ✓

**Part 4**: Define $\phi: H \to HN/N$ by $\phi(h) = hN$.

**Homomorphism**: For $h_1, h_2 \in H$:
$$\phi(h_1 h_2) = (h_1 h_2)N = h_1 N \cdot h_2 N = \phi(h_1)\phi(h_2)$$
✓

**Kernel**: $\ker(\phi) = \{h \in H : hN = N\} = \{h \in H : h \in N\} = H \cap N$. ✓

**Image**: For any $hnN \in HN/N$, we have $hnN = hN = \phi(h)$ since $n \in N$. Thus $\text{Im}(\phi) = HN/N$. ✓

By the First Isomorphism Theorem:
$$H/\ker(\phi) \cong \text{Im}(\phi)$$
$$H/(H \cap N) \cong HN/N$$
$\square$

### Examples

**Example 1**: In $G = \mathbb{Z}_{12}$, let $H = \langle 3 \rangle = \{0, 3, 6, 9\}$ and $N = \langle 4 \rangle = \{0, 4, 8\}$.

Compute:
- $HN$: The subgroup generated by $H \cup N$ = $\langle \gcd(3, 4) \rangle = \langle 1 \rangle = \mathbb{Z}_{12}$
- $H \cap N$: Elements in both = $\{0\}$ (since $\gcd(3, 4) = 1$)

**Second Isomorphism Theorem**:
$$H/(H \cap N) \cong HN/N$$
$$\langle 3 \rangle/\{0\} \cong \mathbb{Z}_{12}/\langle 4 \rangle$$
$$\mathbb{Z}_4 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$$

Wait, this doesn't work! Let me recalculate. In $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$:
- $\langle 3 \rangle = \{0, 3, 6, 9\}$ has order 4
- $\langle 4 \rangle = \{0, 4, 8\}$ has order 3
- $\langle 3 \rangle + \langle 4 \rangle = \langle \gcd(3,4) \rangle = \langle 1 \rangle = \mathbb{Z}_{12}$
- $\langle 3 \rangle \cap \langle 4 \rangle = \langle \text{lcm}(3,4) \rangle = \langle 12 \rangle = \{0\}$

Thus: $\langle 3 \rangle/\{0\} \cong \mathbb{Z}_{12}/\langle 4 \rangle$ gives $\mathbb{Z}_4 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$... Still wrong!

Actually, $|\mathbb{Z}_{12}/\langle 4 \rangle| = 12/3 = 4$, so both sides are isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_4$, not $\mathbb{Z}_3$. Let me verify the LHS:
$|\langle 3 \rangle/(H \cap N)| = 4/1 = 4$. ✓

**Example 2**: In $S_4$, let $H = \langle (1\,2\,3\,4) \rangle$ (cyclic subgroup of order 4) and $N = V_4 = \{e, (1\,2)(3\,4), (1\,3)(2\,4), (1\,4)(2\,3)\}$ (Klein four-group).

Then:
- $|H| = 4$
- $|N| = 4$
- $H \cap N = \{e, (1\,3)(2\,4)\}$ (order 2)
- $|HN| = |H| \cdot |N| / |H \cap N| = 4 \cdot 4 / 2 = 8$

Since $|HN| = 8$ and $HN \subseteq A_4$ (both $H$ and $N$ are in $A_4$), and $|A_4| = 12$, we have $HN \subsetneq A_4$.

**Second Isomorphism Theorem**:
$$H/(H \cap N) \cong HN/N$$
$$|H/(H \cap N)| = 4/2 = 2$$
$$|HN/N| = 8/4 = 2$$

Both quotients are isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_2$. ✓

## Third Isomorphism Theorem

**Third Isomorphism Theorem**: Let $K$ and $N$ be normal subgroups of $G$ with $N \subseteq K$. Then:

1. $K/N$ is a normal subgroup of $G/N$
2. $$(G/N)/(K/N) \cong G/K$$

This theorem says: **"Quotients of quotients are quotients."**

### Proof

Define $\phi: G/N \to G/K$ by $\phi(gN) = gK$.

**Well-defined**: If $g_1 N = g_2 N$, then $g_1^{-1}g_2 \in N \subseteq K$, so $g_1 K = g_2 K$. ✓

**Homomorphism**: $\phi(g_1 N \cdot g_2 N) = \phi((g_1g_2)N) = (g_1g_2)K = g_1 K \cdot g_2 K = \phi(g_1 N)\phi(g_2 N)$. ✓

**Kernel**:
$$\ker(\phi) = \{gN \in G/N : gK = K\} = \{gN : g \in K\} = K/N$$
✓

**Image**: $\phi$ is surjective since for any $gK \in G/K$, we have $\phi(gN) = gK$. ✓

By the First Isomorphism Theorem:
$$(G/N)/\ker(\phi) \cong \text{Im}(\phi)$$
$$(G/N)/(K/N) \cong G/K$$
$\square$

### Examples

**Example 3**: Consider the tower $\mathbb{Z} \supseteq 2\mathbb{Z} \supseteq 6\mathbb{Z}$.

Both $2\mathbb{Z}$ and $6\mathbb{Z}$ are normal in $\mathbb{Z}$ (abelian), and $6\mathbb{Z} \subseteq 2\mathbb{Z}$.

**Third Isomorphism Theorem**:
$$(\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z})/(2\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$$

Left side:
- $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}_6 = \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$
- $2\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} = \{0, 2, 4\}$ (coset of 6 multiples of 2 in $\mathbb{Z}_6$)
- $|\mathbb{Z}_6 / \{0, 2, 4\}| = 6/3 = 2$

Right side: $|\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}| = 2$

Both isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_2$. ✓

**Example 4**: Consider $G = \mathbb{Z}_{24}$ with $N = \langle 8 \rangle$ and $K = \langle 4 \rangle$.

Check: $N = \{0, 8, 16\}$ and $K = \{0, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20\}$, so $N \subseteq K$. ✓

**Third Isomorphism Theorem**:
$$(\mathbb{Z}_{24}/\langle 8 \rangle)/(\langle 4 \rangle/\langle 8 \rangle) \cong \mathbb{Z}_{24}/\langle 4 \rangle$$

Compute sizes:
- $|\mathbb{Z}_{24}/\langle 8 \rangle| = 24/3 = 8$
- $|\langle 4 \rangle/\langle 8 \rangle| = 6/3 = 2$
- LHS: $8/2 = 4$
- RHS: $|\mathbb{Z}_{24}/\langle 4 \rangle| = 24/6 = 4$

Both isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_4$. ✓

## Lattice Isomorphism Theorem (Correspondence Theorem)

**Correspondence Theorem**: Let $N \triangleleft G$ and let $\pi: G \to G/N$ be the natural projection. Then there is a bijection:

$$\{\text{subgroups } H \text{ of } G \text{ containing } N\} \leftrightarrow \{\text{subgroups of } G/N\}$$

given by $H \mapsto H/N = \pi(H)$ with inverse $K \mapsto \pi^{-1}(K)$.

This bijection preserves:
1. **Inclusion**: $H_1 \subseteq H_2 \Leftrightarrow H_1/N \subseteq H_2/N$
2. **Normality**: $H \triangleleft G \Leftrightarrow H/N \triangleleft G/N$
3. **Index**: $[G : H] = [G/N : H/N]$ (when finite)
4. **Generated subgroups**: $\langle H_1, H_2 \rangle/N = \langle H_1/N, H_2/N \rangle$
5. **Intersections**: $(H_1 \cap H_2)/N = (H_1/N) \cap (H_2/N)$

### Proof Sketch

**Forward map well-defined**: If $H \geq N$, then $H/N = \{hN : h \in H\}$ is a subgroup of $G/N$ (verify with subgroup test).

**Inverse map well-defined**: If $K \leq G/N$, then $\pi^{-1}(K) = \{g \in G : gN \in K\}$ contains $N$ and is a subgroup of $G$.

**Bijection**:
- $\pi(\pi^{-1}(K)) = K$ for any $K \leq G/N$
- $\pi^{-1}(\pi(H)) = H$ for any $H \geq N$

The preservation properties follow from the definitions and properties of homomorphisms. $\square$

### Examples

**Example 5**: Consider $G = \mathbb{Z}_6$ and $N = \langle 3 \rangle = \{0, 3\}$.

Subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}_6$ containing $N$:
1. $N = \{0, 3\}$
2. $G = \mathbb{Z}_6$

Subgroups of $G/N = \mathbb{Z}_6/\{0, 3\} \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$:
1. $\{0\}$ (identity)
2. $\mathbb{Z}_3$ (whole group)

Correspondence:
- $\{0, 3\} \leftrightarrow \{0\}$
- $\mathbb{Z}_6 \leftrightarrow \mathbb{Z}_3$

**Example 6**: Consider $G = S_3$ and $N = A_3 = \{e, (1\,2\,3), (1\,3\,2)\}$.

Subgroups of $S_3$ containing $A_3$:
1. $A_3$ itself
2. $S_3$

Subgroups of $S_3/A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}_2$:
1. $\{0\}$
2. $\mathbb{Z}_2$

Correspondence: $A_3 \leftrightarrow \{0\}$ and $S_3 \leftrightarrow \mathbb{Z}_2$.

## Applications

### Application 1: Understanding Quotient Structure

To find all subgroups of $G/N$, enumerate all subgroups of $G$ containing $N$.

**Example**: How many subgroups does $\mathbb{Z}_{12}/\langle 4 \rangle$ have?

Answer: Count subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ containing $\langle 4 \rangle = \{0, 4, 8\}$:
- $\langle 4 \rangle$ itself
- $\langle 2 \rangle = \{0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10\}$
- $\langle 1 \rangle = \mathbb{Z}_{12}$

So $\mathbb{Z}_{12}/\langle 4 \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ has 3 subgroups: $\{0\}$, full group, and one more. Actually $\mathbb{Z}_3$ has only 2 subgroups (it's prime order), so let me recalculate...

$|\mathbb{Z}_{12}/\langle 4 \rangle| = 12/3 = 4$, so it's isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_4$ (cyclic of order 4), which has subgroups $\{0\}, \langle 2 \rangle, \mathbb{Z}_4$.

Corresponding to subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ containing $\{0, 4, 8\}$:
- $\langle 4 \rangle$
- $\langle 2 \rangle$
- $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$

Three subgroups. ✓

### Application 2: Composition Series

The Third Isomorphism Theorem is essential for building composition series:

If $\{e\} = G_0 \triangleleft G_1 \triangleleft \cdots \triangleleft G_n = G$ is a normal series, then:
$$G_{i+1}/G_i \text{ are the composition factors}$$

### Application 3: Simplifying Calculations

Often easier to work in quotient, then lift back via correspondence.

## Summary

**Second Isomorphism Theorem (Diamond)**:
$$H/(H \cap N) \cong HN/N$$
- Relates quotients through intersection and product
- Useful for computing quotient structures

**Third Isomorphism Theorem**:
$$(G/N)/(K/N) \cong G/K \quad \text{for } N \subseteq K \triangleleft G$$
- "Quotients of quotients are quotients"
- Allows iterative quotient construction

**Correspondence Theorem**:
- Bijection between subgroups of $G/N$ and subgroups of $G$ containing $N$
- Preserves inclusion, normality, index
- Essential tool for understanding quotient group structure

Together with the First Isomorphism Theorem, these results provide a complete toolkit for analyzing the relationships between groups, subgroups, normal subgroups, quotients, and homomorphisms. They demonstrate the deep structural connections in group theory.
