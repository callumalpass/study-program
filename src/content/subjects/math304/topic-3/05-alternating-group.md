# The Alternating Group

## Definition

**Definition**: The **alternating group** $A_n$ is the subgroup of $S_n$ consisting of all even permutations:
$$A_n = \{\sigma \in S_n : \text{sgn}(\sigma) = 1\}$$

$A_n$ is the kernel of the sign homomorphism $\text{sgn}: S_n \to \{-1, +1\}$.

## Basic Properties

**Theorem 1**: $A_n$ is a normal subgroup of $S_n$ with index 2.

**Proof**: As the kernel of a homomorphism, $A_n \triangleleft S_n$. Since $\text{sgn}$ is surjective, $|S_n : A_n| = 2$. $\square$

**Order**: $|A_n| = n!/2$ for $n \geq 2$.

**Generators**: $A_n$ is generated by 3-cycles for $n \geq 3$.

## $A_3$ - The Smallest Alternating Group

$|A_3| = 3!/2 = 3$

**Elements**:
- $\text{id}$
- $(1\,2\,3)$
- $(1\,3\,2)$

**Structure**: $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ (cyclic of order 3)

**Cayley Table**:
$$\begin{array}{c|ccc}
\circ & e & (123) & (132) \\
\hline
e & e & (123) & (132) \\
(123) & (123) & (132) & e \\
(132) & (132) & e & (123)
\end{array}$$

## $A_4$ - The Tetrahedral Group

$|A_4| = 24/2 = 12$

**Elements by cycle type**:
- 1 identity
- 8 3-cycles: $(1\,2\,3), (1\,3\,2), (1\,2\,4), (1\,4\,2), (1\,3\,4), (1\,4\,3), (2\,3\,4), (2\,4\,3)$
- 3 double transpositions: $(1\,2)(3\,4), (1\,3)(2\,4), (1\,4)(2\,3)$

**Subgroups**:
- Klein four-group: $V_4 = \{e, (1\,2)(3\,4), (1\,3)(2\,4), (1\,4)(2\,3)\}$
- Four cyclic subgroups of order 3
- Total: 10 subgroups

**Non-simplicity**: $A_4$ is not simple since $V_4 \triangleleft A_4$.

**Geometric interpretation**: Symmetries of a regular tetrahedron.

## $A_5$ - The Icosahedral Group

$|A_5| = 120/2 = 60$

**Elements by cycle type**:
- 1 identity
- 24 5-cycles
- 20 3-cycles
- 15 double transpositions

**Remarkable property**: $A_5$ is **simple** (no nontrivial normal subgroups).

**Proof sketch**: Check that conjugacy classes cannot form normal subgroups except $\{e\}$ and $A_5$. $\square$

**Implications**:
- Smallest non-abelian simple group
- No solvable series â†’ quintic equations unsolvable by radicals (Galois theory)
- Related to icosahedron and dodecahedron symmetries

## Simplicity of $A_n$ for $n \geq 5$

**Theorem**: $A_n$ is simple for all $n \geq 5$.

**Proof outline**:
1. Show $A_n$ is generated by 3-cycles
2. Any normal subgroup containing a 3-cycle contains all 3-cycles
3. Therefore normal subgroups are either $\{e\}$ or $A_n$

$\square$

This is a cornerstone result in group theory.

## Generators of $A_n$

**Theorem**: $A_n$ is generated by 3-cycles.

**Proof**: Every even permutation is a product of an even number of transpositions. Write:
$$(a\,b)(c\,d) = \begin{cases}
(a\,b\,c) & \text{if } b = c \\
(a\,c\,b)(a\,c\,d) & \text{if } b \neq c
\end{cases}$$

Thus every even permutation is a product of 3-cycles. $\square$

**Minimal generators**: $A_n = \langle (1\,2\,3), (1\,2\,4), \ldots, (1\,2\,n) \rangle$.

## Conjugacy in $A_n$

Conjugacy classes in $A_n$ may split from $S_n$.

**Example**: In $S_3$, the 3-cycles $(1\,2\,3)$ and $(1\,3\,2)$ are conjugate. In $A_3$, they remain in the same conjugacy class.

**Example**: In $S_5$, all 5-cycles are conjugate. In $A_5$, they split into two classes:
- $(1\,2\,3\,4\,5), (1\,3\,5\,2\,4), \ldots$ (12 elements)
- $(1\,2\,3\,5\,4), (1\,3\,2\,4\,5), \ldots$ (12 elements)

**Rule**: An $S_n$-conjugacy class splits in $A_n$ iff all elements have odd cycle lengths.

## Subgroups of $A_n$

**$A_4$ subgroups**:
- $\{e\}$, $A_4$
- $V_4$ (normal, order 4)
- Four cyclic subgroups of order 3
- Three cyclic subgroups of order 2

**$A_5$ subgroups**:
- $\{e\}$, $A_5$
- Copies of $A_4, S_3, \mathbb{Z}_5, \mathbb{Z}_3, \mathbb{Z}_2$
- No normal subgroups besides $\{e\}$ and $A_5$ (simplicity)

## Geometric Realizations

**$A_4$**: Rotations of regular tetrahedron (4 vertices, 4 faces, 6 edges)

**$A_5$**: Rotations of icosahedron (12 vertices, 20 faces) or dodecahedron (20 vertices, 12 faces)

These Platonic solids have alternating groups as rotation symmetries.

## Quotient $S_n / A_n$

Since $[S_n : A_n] = 2$:
$$S_n / A_n \cong \mathbb{Z}_2$$

The quotient distinguishes even from odd permutations.

## Applications

**Galois Theory**: Solvability of polynomial equations determined by structure of Galois group. $A_5$ being simple proves quintic unsolvability.

**Rubik's Cube**: Legal positions form even permutations, related to $A_8$ and $A_{12}$.

**Finite Simple Groups**: $A_n$ (for $n \geq 5$) forms infinite family in classification of finite simple groups.

## Summary

| $n$ | $|A_n|$ | Structure | Simple? |
|-----|---------|-----------|---------|
| 1 | 1 | Trivial | No |
| 2 | 1 | Trivial | No |
| 3 | 3 | $\mathbb{Z}_3$ | Yes (prime order) |
| 4 | 12 | Tetrahedral | No ($V_4 \triangleleft A_4$) |
| $\geq 5$ | $n!/2$ | Complex | Yes (theorem) |

The alternating group $A_n$ is fundamental in both group theory and Galois theory.
