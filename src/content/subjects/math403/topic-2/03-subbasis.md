# Subbases for Topologies

## Introduction

While a basis requires that finite intersections stay within the collection (property B2), a **subbasis** has no such requirement. A subbasis is an even more flexible way to generate a topology: we allow finite intersections of subbasis elements to form a basis, and then take arbitrary unions to get all open sets. This provides maximum flexibility in specifying topologies.

## Definition of Subbasis

**Definition 3.1 (Subbasis):** Let $(X, \mathcal{T})$ be a topological space. A collection $\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{T}$ is a **subbasis** (or **subbase**) for $\mathcal{T}$ if the collection of all finite intersections of elements of $\mathcal{S}$ forms a basis for $\mathcal{T}$.

More explicitly, let:
$$\mathcal{B} = \left\{\bigcap_{i=1}^n S_i : S_i \in \mathcal{S}, n \in \mathbb{N}\right\} \cup \{X\}$$

(We include $X$ as the empty intersection.) Then $\mathcal{S}$ is a subbasis if $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{T}$.

**Alternative Definition:** $\mathcal{S}$ is a subbasis for $\mathcal{T}$ if every open set can be written as a union of finite intersections of elements of $\mathcal{S}$.

## The Subbasis Theorem

**Theorem 3.1 (Subbasis Theorem):** Let $X$ be a set and $\mathcal{S}$ be any collection of subsets of $X$ with $\bigcup_{S \in \mathcal{S}} S = X$. Then $\mathcal{S}$ is a subbasis for a unique topology on $X$.

The topology generated by $\mathcal{S}$ consists of all unions of finite intersections of elements of $\mathcal{S}$:
$$\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{S} = \left\{\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} \bigcap_{i=1}^{n_\alpha} S_{\alpha,i} : S_{\alpha,i} \in \mathcal{S}\right\}$$

**Proof:**

Define $\mathcal{B}$ to be the collection of all finite intersections of elements of $\mathcal{S}$ (including $X$ as the empty intersection):
$$\mathcal{B} = \left\{\bigcap_{i=1}^n S_i : S_i \in \mathcal{S}, n \geq 0\right\}$$

We verify $\mathcal{B}$ satisfies the basis criterion B1 and B2.

**B1 (Coverage):** We have $X \in \mathcal{B}$ (empty intersection), so $\bigcup_{B \in \mathcal{B}} B = X$.

**B2 (Intersection Property):** Let $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{B}$. Then:
$$B_1 = \bigcap_{i=1}^n S_i, \quad B_2 = \bigcap_{j=1}^m S'_j$$

where $S_i, S'_j \in \mathcal{S}$. Then:
$$B_1 \cap B_2 = \bigcap_{i=1}^n S_i \cap \bigcap_{j=1}^m S'_j = \bigcap_{k=1}^{n+m} T_k$$

where $T_1, \ldots, T_n = S_1, \ldots, S_n$ and $T_{n+1}, \ldots, T_{n+m} = S'_1, \ldots, S'_m$. This is a finite intersection of elements of $\mathcal{S}$, hence in $\mathcal{B}$.

For any $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$, we can take $B_3 = B_1 \cap B_2 \in \mathcal{B}$ with $x \in B_3 \subseteq B_1 \cap B_2$.

Therefore $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis, and $\mathcal{S}$ is a subbasis for $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{S}$. $\square$

**Important:** Unlike the basis criterion, the subbasis criterion only requires coverage ($\bigcup_{S \in \mathcal{S}} S = X$). No intersection property is needed!

## Examples of Subbases

**Example 3.1 (Standard Topology on $\mathbb{R}$):**

The collection:
$$\mathcal{S} = \{(a, \infty) : a \in \mathbb{R}\} \cup \{(-\infty, b) : b \in \mathbb{R}\}$$

of all rays is a subbasis for the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$.

**Verification:** Finite intersections of rays give:
- $(a, \infty) \cap (c, \infty) = (\max\{a, c\}, \infty)$ (a ray)
- $(-\infty, b) \cap (-\infty, d) = (-\infty, \min\{b, d\})$ (a ray)
- $(a, \infty) \cap (-\infty, b) = (a, b)$ (an open interval if $a < b$, else $\emptyset$)
- More generally, $\bigcap_{i=1}^n (a_i, \infty) \cap \bigcap_{j=1}^m (-\infty, b_j) = (\max_i a_i, \min_j b_j)$ (an interval or empty)

The collection of all such finite intersections includes all open intervals (and rays and $\mathbb{R}$), which form a basis for the standard topology.

**Example 3.2 (Product Topology):**

For topological spaces $(X, \mathcal{T}_X)$ and $(Y, \mathcal{T}_Y)$, the collection:
$$\mathcal{S} = \{U \times Y : U \in \mathcal{T}_X\} \cup \{X \times V : V \in \mathcal{T}_Y\}$$

is a subbasis for the product topology on $X \times Y$.

**Verification:** Finite intersections give:
$$(U_1 \times Y) \cap (U_2 \times Y) = (U_1 \cap U_2) \times Y$$
$$(X \times V_1) \cap (X \times V_2) = X \times (V_1 \cap V_2)$$
$$(U \times Y) \cap (X \times V) = U \times V$$

The collection of all finite intersections includes all sets of the form $U \times V$ where $U \in \mathcal{T}_X$ and $V \in \mathcal{T}_Y$, which is the standard basis for the product topology.

**Example 3.3 (Lower Limit Topology):**

The collection:
$$\mathcal{S} = \{[a, \infty) : a \in \mathbb{R}\} \cup \{(-\infty, b) : b \in \mathbb{R}\}$$

is a subbasis for the lower limit topology on $\mathbb{R}$.

Finite intersections give:
$$[a, \infty) \cap (-\infty, b) = [a, b)$$

These half-open intervals form a basis for the lower limit topology.

**Example 3.4 (Finite Subsets):**

Let $X = \{1, 2, 3\}$ and $\mathcal{S} = \{\{1, 2\}, \{2, 3\}\}$.

Finite intersections:
- $\{1, 2\} \cap \{2, 3\} = \{2\}$
- $\{1, 2\}$, $\{2, 3\}$, $X$ (empty intersection)

So $\mathcal{B} = \{X, \{1, 2\}, \{2, 3\}, \{2\}\}$.

The generated topology is:
$$\mathcal{T} = \{\emptyset, \{2\}, \{1, 2\}, \{2, 3\}, X\}$$

## Comparing Bases and Subbases

**Proposition 3.1:** Every basis is a subbasis.

**Proof:** If $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis, then finite intersections of basis elements can be written as unions of basis elements (by property B2). Therefore the collection of finite intersections includes $\mathcal{B}$ itself, and these generate the same topology. $\square$

**Observation:** A subbasis is generally "smaller" or "simpler" than a basis, making it easier to specify. But working with a subbasis requires taking finite intersections first.

**Example 3.5:** For the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$:
- Basis: all open intervals $(a, b)$ (uncountably many)
- Subbasis: all rays $(a, \infty)$ and $(-\infty, b)$ (uncountably many, but simpler structure)
- Smaller basis: all open intervals $(a, b)$ with $a, b \in \mathbb{Q}$ (countably many)

## Subbasis for Subspace Topology

**Theorem 3.2:** Let $(X, \mathcal{T})$ be a topological space with subbasis $\mathcal{S}$, and let $Y \subseteq X$. Then:
$$\mathcal{S}_Y = \{S \cap Y : S \in \mathcal{S}\}$$

is a subbasis for the subspace topology on $Y$.

**Proof:** The collection of finite intersections of elements of $\mathcal{S}_Y$ is:
$$\mathcal{B}_Y = \left\{\bigcap_{i=1}^n (S_i \cap Y) : S_i \in \mathcal{S}\right\} = \left\{\left(\bigcap_{i=1}^n S_i\right) \cap Y : S_i \in \mathcal{S}\right\}$$

Since $\mathcal{B} = \{\bigcap_{i=1}^n S_i : S_i \in \mathcal{S}\}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{T}$, we have $\mathcal{B}_Y = \{B \cap Y : B \in \mathcal{B}\}$, which is a basis for the subspace topology. $\square$

## Generating Topology from Subbasis: Step-by-Step

Given a subbasis $\mathcal{S}$, the topology is generated in two steps:

**Step 1:** Form the basis $\mathcal{B}$ consisting of all finite intersections of elements of $\mathcal{S}$:
$$\mathcal{B} = \left\{\bigcap_{i=1}^n S_i : S_i \in \mathcal{S}, n \geq 0\right\}$$

**Step 2:** Form the topology $\mathcal{T}$ consisting of all unions of elements of $\mathcal{B}$:
$$\mathcal{T} = \left\{\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} B_\alpha : B_\alpha \in \mathcal{B}\right\}$$

**Example 3.6:** Let $X = \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathcal{S} = \{(0, \infty), (-\infty, 1)\}$.

**Step 1:** Finite intersections:
- $X$ (empty intersection)
- $(0, \infty)$
- $(-\infty, 1)$
- $(0, \infty) \cap (-\infty, 1) = (0, 1)$

So $\mathcal{B} = \{X, (0, \infty), (-\infty, 1), (0, 1)\}$.

**Step 2:** All unions:
$$\mathcal{T} = \{\emptyset, (0, 1), (0, \infty), (-\infty, 1), (0, \infty) \cup (-\infty, 1), X\}$$

Note that $(0, \infty) \cup (-\infty, 1) = \mathbb{R} \setminus [1, 0] = \mathbb{R}$ (since $0 < 1$). Actually, $(0, \infty) \cup (-\infty, 1) = (-\infty, 1) \cup (0, \infty)$ covers everything except... wait, this covers all of $\mathbb{R}$ since $0 < 1$. Let me reconsider.

$(0, \infty) = \{x : x > 0\}$ and $(-\infty, 1) = \{x : x < 1\}$. Their union is $\{x : x > 0 \text{ or } x < 1\} = \mathbb{R}$ (every real number is either $> 0$ or $< 1$). So the topology is:
$$\mathcal{T} = \{\emptyset, (0, 1), (0, \infty), (-\infty, 1), X\}$$

## Minimal Subbases

**Definition 3.2 (Minimal Subbasis):** A subbasis $\mathcal{S}$ is **minimal** if no proper subset of $\mathcal{S}$ is a subbasis for the same topology.

**Example 3.7:** The subbasis $\mathcal{S} = \{(a, \infty), (-\infty, b) : a, b \in \mathbb{R}\}$ for the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$ is not minimal - we can remove some rays and still generate the same topology using rational endpoints.

**Proposition 3.2:** A subbasis $\mathcal{S}$ is minimal if and only if for every $S \in \mathcal{S}$, the element $S$ cannot be written as a union of finite intersections of elements of $\mathcal{S} \setminus \{S\}$.

**Proof:** If $S$ can be so written, then $\mathcal{S} \setminus \{S\}$ generates the same topology, so $\mathcal{S}$ is not minimal. Conversely, if $\mathcal{S}$ is not minimal, some proper subset generates the same topology, so some element is redundant. $\square$

## Applications of Subbases

**Application 3.1 (Defining New Topologies):** Subbases provide an easy way to define topologies - just specify simple open sets and take finite intersections then arbitrary unions.

**Application 3.2 (Weak Topologies):** In functional analysis, the weak topology on a vector space is defined using a subbasis consisting of sets of the form $\{x : |f(x)| < \epsilon\}$ for linear functionals $f$.

**Application 3.3 (Initial Topologies):** Given a family of functions $f_\alpha: X \to Y_\alpha$ where each $Y_\alpha$ has a topology, the **initial topology** on $X$ is generated by the subbasis:
$$\mathcal{S} = \{f_\alpha^{-1}(U) : U \text{ open in } Y_\alpha\}$$

This makes all the $f_\alpha$ continuous and is the coarsest topology with this property.

## Subbasis vs Basis: When to Use Each

**Use a subbasis when:**
- The natural "basic open sets" don't satisfy B2
- You want to minimize the description of the topology
- You're defining an initial topology from functions

**Use a basis when:**
- The collection naturally satisfies B2
- You need to work directly with basic open sets
- You're proving properties of the topology

**Example 3.8:** For the product topology on $X \times Y$:
- Subbasis: $\{U \times Y, X \times V : U \in \mathcal{T}_X, V \in \mathcal{T}_Y\}$ (simpler to write)
- Basis: $\{U \times V : U \in \mathcal{T}_X, V \in \mathcal{T}_Y\}$ (easier to work with)

## Alexander Subbase Theorem

A deep theorem about subbases and compactness:

**Theorem 3.3 (Alexander Subbase Theorem):** A topological space $X$ is compact if and only if every cover of $X$ by subbasis elements has a finite subcover.

This is a powerful tool for proving compactness - we only need to check covers by subbasis elements, not all open covers. We'll explore this more when studying compactness.

## Summary

Key concepts:

1. **Subbasis:** Collection $\mathcal{S}$ where finite intersections form a basis
2. **Subbasis Theorem:** Any collection covering $X$ generates a topology
3. **Simpler than basis:** No intersection property required (only coverage)
4. **Generation:** Topology = all unions of finite intersections of subbasis elements
5. **Examples:** Rays for $\mathbb{R}$, projections for products
6. **Applications:** Weak topologies, initial topologies, minimal descriptions

Subbases provide maximum flexibility in generating topologies, often with simpler descriptions than bases. They're essential for product topologies, quotient topologies, and weak topologies in functional analysis.
