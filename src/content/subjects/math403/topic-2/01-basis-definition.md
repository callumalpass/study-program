# Definition of a Basis

## Introduction

A topology can have infinitely many open sets, making it cumbersome to specify explicitly. A **basis** provides a more economical description: it's a collection of "basic" open sets from which all other open sets can be built as unions. This concept is analogous to a basis in linear algebra, where all vectors are built from basis vectors.

## Definition of a Basis

**Definition 1.1 (Basis):** Let $(X, \mathcal{T})$ be a topological space. A collection $\mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{T}$ of open sets is a **basis** for $\mathcal{T}$ if every open set in $\mathcal{T}$ can be written as a union of sets in $\mathcal{B}$.

Formally, $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{T}$ if for every $U \in \mathcal{T}$, there exists a subcollection $\{B_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I} \subseteq \mathcal{B}$ such that:
$$U = \bigcup_{\alpha \in I} B_\alpha$$

**Alternative Characterization:** $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{T}$ if and only if for every $x \in U$ where $U \in \mathcal{T}$, there exists $B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B \subseteq U$.

## Examples of Bases

**Example 1.1 (Standard Basis for $\mathbb{R}$):**

The collection of all open intervals:
$$\mathcal{B} = \{(a, b) : a, b \in \mathbb{R}, a < b\}$$

is a basis for the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$.

**Verification:** Let $U$ be open in $\mathbb{R}$. For each $x \in U$, there exists $\epsilon_x > 0$ such that $(x - \epsilon_x, x + \epsilon_x) \subseteq U$ (by definition of open set). Then:
$$U = \bigcup_{x \in U} (x - \epsilon_x, x + \epsilon_x)$$

Each $(x - \epsilon_x, x + \epsilon_x) \in \mathcal{B}$, so $U$ is a union of basis elements.

**Example 1.2 (Rational Endpoints):**

The collection:
$$\mathcal{B}_\mathbb{Q} = \{(a, b) : a, b \in \mathbb{Q}, a < b\}$$

of open intervals with rational endpoints is also a basis for the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}$.

**Verification:** Let $U$ be open and $x \in U$. There exists $(x - \epsilon, x + \epsilon) \subseteq U$. Choose rationals $a, b$ with $x - \epsilon < a < x < b < x + \epsilon$. Then $(a, b) \in \mathcal{B}_\mathbb{Q}$ and $x \in (a, b) \subseteq (x - \epsilon, x + \epsilon) \subseteq U$.

This basis is **countable**, showing that $\mathbb{R}$ has a countable basis (making it **second-countable**).

**Example 1.3 (Basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$):**

The collection of all open rectangles:
$$\mathcal{B} = \{(a, b) \times (c, d) : a, b, c, d \in \mathbb{R}\}$$

is a basis for the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}^2$.

Alternatively, the collection of all open disks:
$$\mathcal{B}' = \{B((x, y), r) : x, y, r \in \mathbb{R}, r > 0\}$$

is also a basis for the standard topology on $\mathbb{R}^2$.

**Example 1.4 (Discrete Topology):**

For any set $X$ with the discrete topology, the collection:
$$\mathcal{B} = \{\{x\} : x \in X\}$$

of all singletons is a basis. Every subset $A \subseteq X$ can be written as $A = \bigcup_{x \in A} \{x\}$.

**Example 1.5 (Trivial Topology):**

For the indiscrete topology $\mathcal{T} = \{\emptyset, X\}$, the collection $\mathcal{B} = \{X\}$ is a basis (taking the convention that $\emptyset$ is the empty union).

## The Topology Generated by a Basis

Given a collection of sets, we can ask: is it a basis for some topology? And if so, which topology?

**Definition 1.2 (Generated Topology):** Let $X$ be a set and $\mathcal{B}$ be a collection of subsets of $X$. The **topology generated by $\mathcal{B}$** is:
$$\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B} = \left\{\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} B_\alpha : B_\alpha \in \mathcal{B}\right\}$$

In other words, $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$ consists of all unions (including empty unions, giving $\emptyset$) of sets in $\mathcal{B}$.

**Question:** When is $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$ actually a topology?

**Theorem 1.1 (Basis Criterion):** Let $X$ be a set and $\mathcal{B}$ be a collection of subsets of $X$. Then $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for some topology on $X$ if and only if:

1. **B1 (Coverage):** $\bigcup_{B \in \mathcal{B}} B = X$ (every point is in some basis element)
2. **B2 (Intersection Property):** For any $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{B}$ and any $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$, there exists $B_3 \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B_3 \subseteq B_1 \cap B_2$

When these conditions hold, $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$.

**Proof:**

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for some topology $\mathcal{T}$ on $X$.

- **B1:** Since $X \in \mathcal{T}$ and $X = \bigcup_{B \in \mathcal{B}} B$ (every open set is a union of basis elements), we have coverage.

- **B2:** Let $B_1, B_2 \in \mathcal{B}$ and $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$. Since $B_1, B_2$ are open (being in $\mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{T}$), their intersection $B_1 \cap B_2$ is open. Since $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis, for $x \in B_1 \cap B_2$, there exists $B_3 \in \mathcal{B}$ with $x \in B_3 \subseteq B_1 \cap B_2$.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose B1 and B2 hold. We show $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$ is a topology.

- **T1:** By B1, $X = \bigcup_{B \in \mathcal{B}} B \in \mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$. The empty union gives $\emptyset \in \mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$.

- **T2:** Let $\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in I}$ be a collection in $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$. For each $\alpha$, write $U_\alpha = \bigcup_{\beta \in I_\alpha} B_{\alpha,\beta}$ where $B_{\alpha,\beta} \in \mathcal{B}$. Then:
  $$\bigcup_{\alpha \in I} U_\alpha = \bigcup_{\alpha \in I} \bigcup_{\beta \in I_\alpha} B_{\alpha,\beta}$$
  This is a union of sets in $\mathcal{B}$, so it's in $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$.

- **T3:** Let $U_1, \ldots, U_n \in \mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$. Write $U_i = \bigcup_{\alpha_i \in I_i} B_{\alpha_i}$ where $B_{\alpha_i} \in \mathcal{B}$.

  We need to show $\bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i \in \mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$. Let $x \in \bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i$. For each $i$, there exists $\alpha_i$ such that $x \in B_{\alpha_i} \subseteq U_i$.

  By B2 applied repeatedly, there exists $B \in \mathcal{B}$ such that $x \in B \subseteq \bigcap_{i=1}^n B_{\alpha_i} \subseteq \bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i$.

  Therefore:
  $$\bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i = \bigcup_{x \in \bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i} B_x$$
  where for each $x$, we've chosen $B_x \in \mathcal{B}$ with $x \in B_x \subseteq \bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i$. This is a union of sets in $\mathcal{B}$, so it's in $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$. $\square$

## Examples: Verifying the Basis Criterion

**Example 1.6:** Let $\mathcal{B} = \{(a, b) : a, b \in \mathbb{R}, a < b\}$ on $\mathbb{R}$.

- **B1:** $\bigcup_{a,b \in \mathbb{R}} (a, b) = \mathbb{R}$ ✓
- **B2:** Let $(a, b), (c, d) \in \mathcal{B}$ and $x \in (a, b) \cap (c, d)$. If $(a,b) \cap (c, d) = \emptyset$, we're done. Otherwise, $(a, b) \cap (c, d) = (\max\{a, c\}, \min\{b, d\})$, which is an open interval (and thus in $\mathcal{B}$) containing $x$ and contained in the intersection. ✓

**Example 1.7 (Non-basis):** Let $X = \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathcal{B} = \{[a, b) : a, b \in \mathbb{R}, a < b\}$ (half-open intervals).

- **B1:** $\bigcup_{a,b} [a, b) = \mathbb{R}$ ✓
- **B2:** Consider $[0, 2)$ and $[1, 3)$. Their intersection is $[1, 2)$. For $x = 1 \in [1, 2)$, we need $B \in \mathcal{B}$ with $1 \in B \subseteq [1, 2)$. Any such $B$ has the form $[a, b)$ with $a \leq 1 < b \leq 2$. But if $a < 1$, then $[a, b) \not\subseteq [1, 2)$. If $a = 1$, then $B = [1, b) \subseteq [1, 2)$ works. ✓

Actually, $\mathcal{B}$ does satisfy B2! Let's verify more carefully: for $[a, b) \cap [c, d) \neq \emptyset$, we have $[a, b) \cap [c, d) = [\max\{a, c\}, \min\{b, d\})$, which is in $\mathcal{B}$ (or empty). So $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for the **lower limit topology**.

**Example 1.8 (Non-basis):** Let $X = \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathcal{B} = \{[a, b] : a, b \in \mathbb{R}, a < b\}$ (closed intervals).

- **B1:** $\bigcup_{a, b} [a, b] = \mathbb{R}$ ✓
- **B2:** Consider $[0, 2]$ and $[1, 3]$. Their intersection is $[1, 2]$. For $x = 1$, we need $B \in \mathcal{B}$ with $1 \in B \subseteq [1, 2]$. The only closed intervals containing $1$ and contained in $[1, 2]$ are $[1, b]$ for $b \in [1, 2]$. So $B = [1, 1] = \{1\}$ works... but $\{1\}$ is not of the form $[a, b]$ with $a < b$.

If we allow $a = b$, then $[1, 1] = \{1\} \in \mathcal{B}$. But the issue is that closed intervals don't generally work because the topology they generate includes all arbitrary unions, which might not be closed.

Actually, the real issue is: the intersection $[0, 1] \cap [1, 2] = \{1\}$ is not a closed interval of the form $[a, b]$ with $a < b$. So B2 fails. $\mathcal{B}$ is not a basis.

## Uniqueness of Generated Topology

**Proposition 1.1:** If $\mathcal{B}$ satisfies B1 and B2, the topology $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B}$ it generates is unique and is the coarsest topology containing $\mathcal{B}$.

**Proof:** Any topology containing $\mathcal{B}$ must contain all unions of sets in $\mathcal{B}$ (by axiom T2). Therefore $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{T}$ for any such topology $\mathcal{T}$. $\square$

## Local Basis

**Definition 1.3 (Local Basis):** Let $(X, \mathcal{T})$ be a topological space and $x \in X$. A collection $\mathcal{B}_x$ of open sets containing $x$ is a **local basis** (or **neighborhood basis**) at $x$ if for every open set $U$ containing $x$, there exists $B \in \mathcal{B}_x$ such that $B \subseteq U$.

**Example 1.9:** In $\mathbb{R}$ with the standard topology:
- $\mathcal{B}_0 = \{(-1/n, 1/n) : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ is a local basis at $0$
- $\mathcal{B}_x = \{(x - 1/n, x + 1/n) : n \in \mathbb{N}\}$ is a local basis at $x$

**Proposition 1.2:** $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{T}$ if and only if for every $x \in X$, the collection $\mathcal{B}_x = \{B \in \mathcal{B} : x \in B\}$ is a local basis at $x$.

**Proof:** Exercise. $\square$

## Comparing Bases

**Definition 1.4:** If $\mathcal{B}_1$ and $\mathcal{B}_2$ are bases generating topologies $\mathcal{T}_1$ and $\mathcal{T}_2$ respectively, we say $\mathcal{B}_1$ is **finer** than $\mathcal{B}_2$ if $\mathcal{T}_1 \supseteq \mathcal{T}_2$.

**Proposition 1.3 (Basis Comparison):** Let $\mathcal{B}_1$ and $\mathcal{B}_2$ be bases for topologies $\mathcal{T}_1$ and $\mathcal{T}_2$ on $X$. Then $\mathcal{T}_1 \supseteq \mathcal{T}_2$ (i.e., $\mathcal{T}_1$ is finer) if and only if for every $x \in X$ and every $B_2 \in \mathcal{B}_2$ with $x \in B_2$, there exists $B_1 \in \mathcal{B}_1$ such that $x \in B_1 \subseteq B_2$.

**Proof:**

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $\mathcal{T}_1 \supseteq \mathcal{T}_2$. Let $x \in B_2 \in \mathcal{B}_2$. Since $B_2 \in \mathcal{T}_2 \subseteq \mathcal{T}_1$ and $\mathcal{B}_1$ is a basis for $\mathcal{T}_1$, there exists $B_1 \in \mathcal{B}_1$ with $x \in B_1 \subseteq B_2$.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose the condition holds. Let $U \in \mathcal{T}_2$. Write $U = \bigcup_{\alpha} B_{2,\alpha}$ where $B_{2,\alpha} \in \mathcal{B}_2$. For each $x \in U$, there exists $\alpha$ with $x \in B_{2,\alpha}$. By hypothesis, there exists $B_1 \in \mathcal{B}_1$ with $x \in B_1 \subseteq B_{2,\alpha} \subseteq U$. Thus $U$ is a union of sets in $\mathcal{B}_1$, so $U \in \mathcal{T}_1$. Therefore $\mathcal{T}_2 \subseteq \mathcal{T}_1$. $\square$

## Summary

Key concepts:

1. **Basis:** A collection $\mathcal{B}$ such that every open set is a union of sets in $\mathcal{B}$
2. **Basis Criterion:** $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis iff it satisfies B1 (coverage) and B2 (intersection property)
3. **Generated Topology:** $\mathcal{T}_\mathcal{B} = \{\bigcup_\alpha B_\alpha : B_\alpha \in \mathcal{B}\}$
4. **Local Basis:** Collection of open sets at a point that "approximates" all neighborhoods
5. **Comparison:** Bases can be compared via the topologies they generate

Bases provide an efficient way to specify and work with topologies, especially when the full topology is too large to describe explicitly. They're fundamental for constructing new topologies and proving topological properties.
