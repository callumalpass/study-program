# The Symmetric Group

## Definition and Structure

**Definition**: The **symmetric group** $S_n$ is the group of all permutations of the set $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ under function composition.

**Properties**:
- **Order**: $|S_n| = n!$
- **Identity**: $\text{id}(i) = i$ for all $i$
- **Non-abelian** for $n \geq 3$
- **Universal**: Every finite group is isomorphic to a subgroup of some $S_n$ (Cayley's Theorem)

## Verification that $S_n$ is a Group

**Closure**: The composition of two bijections is a bijection. ✓

**Associativity**: Function composition is associative. ✓

**Identity**: The identity function is in $S_n$. ✓

**Inverses**: Every bijection has an inverse bijection. ✓

Therefore, $(S_n, \circ)$ is a group.

## $S_3$ in Detail

The smallest non-abelian symmetric group.

**Elements**: Let's denote permutations both ways:
- $e = \text{id} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ (identity)
- $\sigma_1 = (12) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ (swap 1,2)
- $\sigma_2 = (13) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ (swap 1,3)
- $\sigma_3 = (23) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 3 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ (swap 2,3)
- $\sigma_4 = (123) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ (cycle 1→2→3)
- $\sigma_5 = (132) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ (cycle 1→3→2)

**Cayley Table for $S_3$**:
$$\begin{array}{c|cccccc}
\circ & e & (12) & (13) & (23) & (123) & (132) \\
\hline
e & e & (12) & (13) & (23) & (123) & (132) \\
(12) & (12) & e & (123) & (132) & (13) & (23) \\
(13) & (13) & (132) & e & (123) & (23) & (12) \\
(23) & (23) & (123) & (132) & e & (12) & (13) \\
(123) & (123) & (23) & (12) & (13) & (132) & e \\
(132) & (132) & (13) & (23) & (12) & e & (123)
\end{array}$$

**Non-commutativity**: $(12)(23) = (123)$ but $(23)(12) = (132) \neq (123)$.

**Subgroups of $S_3$**:
- $\{e\}$ (trivial)
- $\{e, (12)\}$, $\{e, (13)\}$, $\{e, (23)\}$ (order 2)
- $\{e, (123), (132)\} \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ (order 3)
- $S_3$ itself

**Isomorphism**: $S_3 \cong D_3$ (dihedral group of triangle).

## Generators of $S_n$

**Theorem**: $S_n$ is generated by transpositions (2-cycles).

In fact, $S_n$ can be generated by the $n-1$ adjacent transpositions:
$$(1\,2), (2\,3), (3\,4), \ldots, (n-1\,n)$$

**Proof sketch**: Any permutation can be written as a product of transpositions (covered in later sections). $\square$

**Example for $S_3$**: Generated by $(1\,2)$ and $(2\,3)$:
- $e$ = identity
- $(1\,2)$ = given
- $(2\,3)$ = given
- $(1\,2)(2\,3) = (1\,2\,3)$
- $(2\,3)(1\,2) = (1\,3\,2)$
- $(1\,2)(2\,3)(1\,2) = (1\,3)$

All 6 elements can be expressed.

**Minimal Generation**: $S_n$ can be generated by just 2 elements: $(1\,2)$ and $(1\,2\,\ldots\,n)$.

## Center of $S_n$

**Theorem**: $Z(S_n) = \{e\}$ for $n \geq 3$.

**Proof**: The center consists of permutations that commute with all others. For $n \geq 3$, only the identity has this property. $\square$

This means $S_n$ is "maximally non-abelian" for $n \geq 3$.

## Order of Elements in $S_n$

The order of a permutation equals the least common multiple of the lengths of its disjoint cycles (proven later).

**Examples in $S_5$**:
- $|(1\,2)| = 2$
- $|(1\,2\,3)| = 3$
- $|(1\,2\,3\,4\,5)| = 5$
- $|(1\,2)(3\,4\,5)| = \text{lcm}(2, 3) = 6$
- $|(1\,2)(3\,4)| = \text{lcm}(2, 2) = 2$

**Maximum Order**: The maximum order of an element in $S_n$ grows approximately like $e^{\sqrt{n \ln n}}$ (Landau's function).

For small $n$:
- Max order in $S_3$: 3
- Max order in $S_4$: 4
- Max order in $S_5$: 6 (achieved by $(1\,2)(3\,4\,5)$)
- Max order in $S_6$: 6
- Max order in $S_7$: 12

## Conjugacy in $S_n$

Two permutations are conjugate if they have the same cycle structure.

**Example**: In $S_4$:
- $(1\,2)$, $(1\,3)$, $(1\,4)$, $(2\,3)$, $(2\,4)$, $(3\,4)$ all conjugate (all transpositions)
- $(1\,2\,3)$, $(1\,2\,4)$, $(1\,3\,4)$, $(2\,3\,4)$, etc. all conjugate (all 3-cycles)

Conjugacy classes partition $S_n$ by cycle type.

## Normal Subgroups of $S_n$

**Theorem**: For $n \geq 5$, the only normal subgroups of $S_n$ are $\{e\}$, $A_n$ (alternating group), and $S_n$ itself.

For $n \leq 4$, there are additional normal subgroups.

**$S_4$ Example**: Has a normal subgroup $V_4 = \{e, (1\,2)(3\,4), (1\,3)(2\,4), (1\,4)(2\,3)\}$ (Klein four-group).

## Embedding Groups into $S_n$

**Cayley's Theorem**: Every finite group $G$ of order $n$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of $S_n$.

**Proof sketch**: Each element $g \in G$ defines a permutation $\sigma_g: G \to G$ by $\sigma_g(h) = gh$ (left multiplication). The map $g \mapsto \sigma_g$ is an injective homomorphism $G \to S_n$. $\square$

**Example**: $\mathbb{Z}_3 \hookrightarrow S_3$:
- $0 \mapsto e$
- $1 \mapsto (1\,2\,3)$
- $2 \mapsto (1\,3\,2)$

This shows the cyclic subgroup of order 3 in $S_3$.

## Representation as Permutation Matrices

Each element of $S_n$ corresponds to an $n \times n$ permutation matrix (one 1 per row/column, rest 0s).

**Example**: $(1\,2\,3) \in S_3$:
$$P = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

The determinant of a permutation matrix is $\pm 1$ (sign of the permutation).

## Applications

**Rubik's Cube**: The group of Rubik's cube moves is a subgroup of $S_{48}$ (48 facets).

**Card Shuffling**: A shuffle is a permutation in $S_{52}$.

**Algorithm Complexity**: Sorting algorithms can be analyzed via permutations.

**Chemistry**: Molecular symmetries use permutation groups.

## Subgroup Structure

$S_n$ has rich subgroup structure:
- Cyclic subgroups $\langle \sigma \rangle$ for each $\sigma \in S_n$
- Alternating group $A_n$ (index 2 subgroup)
- Stabilizer subgroups (fix certain elements)
- Young subgroups (permute within blocks)

## Summary

Key facts about $S_n$:
- Order $n!$, non-abelian for $n \geq 3$
- Generated by transpositions
- Contains every finite group (Cayley)
- Trivial center for $n \geq 3$
- Conjugacy classes determined by cycle type
- Simple for $n \geq 5$ except for $A_n$

The symmetric group is fundamental to understanding all finite groups.
